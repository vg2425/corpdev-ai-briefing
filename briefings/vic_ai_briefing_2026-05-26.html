Anthropic set to close $30B round at $900B+ valuation this week, overtaking OpenAI as the most valuable AI lab

Bloomberg first reported the round on May 12, with confirmation on May 22 that the term sheet could be signed as soon as the week of May 26. Sequoia, Dragoneer, Altimeter and Greenoaks each commit roughly $2B as co leads, with Founders Fund and General Catalyst also participating.

▶

This is Anthropic's second $30B round of 2026. The February round closed at a $380B post money mark, so the headline valuation has more than doubled in roughly three months. If the deal lands at the upper end of the band, Anthropic surpasses OpenAI's $852B March mark for the first time, fully inverting the relative positioning the two labs held for most of 2024 and 2025.

The funding sits on top of revenue projections that are doing most of the work. Anthropic informed investors it expects Q2 revenue of about $10.9B, more than double Q1 ($4.8B) and likely the first quarter of operating profit in the company's history (story 02 below). I would push back on the "first profitable quarter" framing slightly: this is operating profit on Anthropic accounting, not a clean GAAP net income print, and it depends on how scheduled compute commitments are amortized. Worth verifying once a formal disclosure exists.

Cross source check: Bloomberg, Yahoo Finance, Crunchbase News, TechTimes, MacObserver, KuCoin, TradingKey, AIThinkerLab and StartupHub all converge on the same headline numbers. The $2B per co lead figure is reported by Bloomberg and Crunchbase News. I have not seen a signed term sheet so this should still be characterized as imminent rather than closed.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

Anthropic at $900B with explicit "difficulties with compute" comments from Dario Amodei (story 07 below) is the cleanest possible bull case for AI neocloud demand. Anthropic's Q1 demand reportedly hit 80x annualized growth versus a planned 10x. Each incremental gigawatt of contracted power that CoreWeave can land into 2027 is competing for exactly this kind of workload. The flip side: a $900B private mark concentrates customer risk, and Anthropic is now exploring alternative silicon paths (Microsoft Maia, story 07), which is the early signal worth tracking.

Anthropic projects $10.9B Q2 revenue and its first ever quarterly operating profit, well ahead of prior guidance

CNBC and Yahoo Finance reported on May 20 that Anthropic told investors it now expects Q2 revenue of roughly $10.9B versus $4.8B in Q1, with about $559M in operating profit. Last summer the company had guided full year profitability no earlier than 2028.

▶

The single quarter revenue figure, if it lands, would exceed Anthropic's entire 2025 revenue and is roughly an order of magnitude above where the company started 2024. The driver is Claude API and Claude Code growth, with enterprise seats and developer agent workloads compounding. The CNBC source describes the figure as "told investors" rather than disclosed, so I would treat the $10.9B as directional until either the IPO filing or an audited financial release lands.

The $559M operating profit number is more interesting than the revenue number. It implies roughly 5 percent operating margin, which is consistent with a company that is still scaling compute commitments aggressively. Several outlets (Quasa, in particular) have flagged that the "operating profit" framing excludes major scheduled compute commitments and is therefore a soft definition. That is a fair pushback worth carrying forward.

Cross source check: WSJ via CNBC, Yahoo Finance, Investing.com, Dataconomy, Newsbytes, Winbuzzer and TechBriefly all align on the same figures. The pushback on definitions comes from Quasa and a Medium analysis. Treat as well sourced but not yet audited.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

Anthropic's revenue scale and gross margin trajectory are the closest available proxy for the unit economics of large scale Claude inference, which is now over 50 percent of CoreWeave's platform activity (per Q1 commentary). If Anthropic is generating operating profit, the inference workload that CoreWeave is running for OpenAI, Microsoft and the rest of the customer book is also more likely to be sustainable at current pricing. The risk to read carefully: if scheduled compute spend later in 2026 erodes the profit print, the read across to GPU cloud pricing power weakens.

🔥 High Signal Research Models 7 sources

OpenAI says an internal reasoning model autonomously disproved an 80 year old Erdős conjecture in discrete geometry

OpenAI, TechCrunch and Interesting Engineering published on May 20 that a general purpose reasoning model produced a disproof of the planar unit distance conjecture posed by Paul Erdős in 1946. The construction reportedly uses Golod Shafarevich theory and infinite class field towers, achieving a polynomial improvement over the square grid bound.

▶

The technical claim, as reported, is that the model produced a family of constructions achieving n^(1+δ) unit distance pairs for some fixed δ > 0. A subsequent refinement by Princeton mathematician Will Sawin is cited at δ ≈ 0.014. I cannot independently verify the proof, and I would caveat that "AI disproves Erdős conjecture" headlines have a long history of overstating what was actually new in the construction. The model output reportedly required human mathematician verification and refinement, which is the norm for any AI assisted result of this kind.

What is more interesting than the math is the system claim. OpenAI says the model was a general purpose reasoning system not specifically trained for mathematics or targeted at this problem, and was tested across a broader set of Erdős problems. If that holds up, it is the cleanest evidence to date that frontier reasoning models can produce genuinely novel mathematical output rather than retrieving or interpolating known results. Tim Gowers's reported comment that this is "a milestone in AI mathematics" is plausible but I have not verified it against a primary source.

Cross source check: OpenAI's own blog post, TechCrunch, Interesting Engineering, ExplainX, AItoolly, Autogpt and Digg all describe the same construction. Coverage attributes the result to an unnamed "internal reasoning model" with no public model card. Treat the headline claim as well sourced and the technical details as needing independent peer review.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

The relevant signal is not the math, it is the compute profile of the workload. OpenAI is publicly claiming that long horizon, agentic reasoning runs over open mathematical problems produce frontier research. Those workloads have different inference economics than chat: long context, very high token output per query, expensive search and verification loops. If this becomes a routine research pattern at the frontier, the inference fleet shape (more KV cache, more long context inference, less batch efficiency) drifts toward the configurations CoreWeave's Rubin CPX class deployments are built around.

🔥 High Signal Policy 6 sources

Pope Leo XIV publishes Magnifica Humanitas on May 25, framing AI as the Rerum Novarum moment of our era

The Vatican confirmed publication of Leo XIV's first encyclical on May 25, dated May 15 to mark the 135th anniversary of Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum. The document warns against systems that "reduce people to data points, labor units, or instruments of control," and engages directly with labor, education, warfare, family life and public truth in the AI era.

▶

This is the first papal encyclical to take artificial intelligence as its primary frame, and the choice of title and signing date is a deliberate analogy: Rerum Novarum in 1891 articulated Catholic social teaching for the industrial revolution and is still the canonical anchor for the Church's labor and economic doctrine. By signing Magnifica Humanitas on May 15 (the Rerum Novarum anniversary) and publishing it on May 25, Leo XIV is explicitly positioning AI in that frame.

The substantive content, per Vatican News and the National Catholic Register, runs along three lines: human dignity (people are not data points or labor units), solidarity (AI must not concentrate power) and the common good (development should reach low and middle income countries, not just the labs that build the models). The encyclical also includes a separately notable apology for the Holy See's historical role in legitimizing slavery, which is dominating some of the secular coverage independent of the AI framing.

Cross source check: vatican.va, Vatican News, the National Catholic Register, Ascension Press, NBC News and the USCCB statement all align on the publication date and core content. I have not read the full text yet, so any specific policy recommendations cited in secondary coverage should be verified against the vatican.va primary text before being treated as canonical.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

Less direct than the financial stories, but real. The encyclical will be widely cited as a moral authority anchor in AI policy debates in the EU, the US Congress and Catholic majority countries (Brazil, the Philippines, Mexico, Poland). For a US listed infrastructure company, the relevant downstream watch item is procurement and ESG screens: large pension funds and university endowments that take Catholic social teaching as a reference point may apply the encyclical's "concentration of power" language to AI infrastructure positions over the next 12 months.

Anthropic in early talks to rent Microsoft Maia 200 AI chips on Azure, the first major external Maia deployment

CNBC and Bloomberg confirmed on May 21 that Anthropic and Microsoft are in early talks for Anthropic to run workloads on Microsoft's Maia 200 silicon hosted on Azure. The Information broke the story. No deal is signed.

▶

Three facts to anchor on. First, Maia 200 was announced by Microsoft in January 2026 but has not been generally available to Azure customers, so any Anthropic deployment would be effectively a first reference customer. Second, Nadella claimed on the April earnings call that Maia 200 offers "over 30 percent improved tokens per dollar" versus the rest of Microsoft's fleet, which is the kind of efficiency number that would justify an Anthropic look. Third, Anthropic is already running primarily on Trainium at AWS and on TPUs at Google, plus Nvidia GPUs across multiple clouds. Adding Maia would make it the only frontier lab running on four distinct silicon architectures in parallel.

The contextual driver is straightforward: Dario Amodei has publicly said Anthropic is compute constrained, with Q1 demand reportedly running 80x annualized versus a 10x plan. The strategic story is more interesting: Microsoft has been the most behind on its in house silicon among the three big clouds, and landing Anthropic would meaningfully accelerate Maia's roadmap and credibility. The reciprocal point is what Anthropic gets in return, which is presumably either price concession or supply commitments outside Maia.

Cross source check: Bloomberg, CNBC, GuruFocus, Yahoo Finance and Winbuzzer all confirm the talks and reference The Information's original reporting. The 30 percent tokens per dollar figure is Microsoft's own claim from the April earnings call and has not been independently benchmarked.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

This is the most directly relevant story in this briefing for CoreWeave positioning. The thesis that AI labs are locked into Nvidia GPUs in the medium term is the foundation of the AI neocloud bull case. Anthropic publicly broadening its silicon set, on top of Trainium and TPU, is the strongest signal yet that frontier labs are actively de risking their compute supply chains. CoreWeave's counter argument is software (CUDA, ecosystem) plus speed of deployment versus hyperscaler procurement timelines, and this deal would be a useful pricing comparison point if Microsoft starts publishing Maia rate cards.

Trump pulls planned AI safety executive order after direct lobbying from Zuckerberg, Musk and David Sacks

Axios, NPR and CNN reported between May 20 and May 22 that the White House postponed an executive order that would have established a voluntary 90 day pre release federal review for frontier AI models. Trump said he "didn't like certain aspects" of the order; reporting credits direct calls from Zuckerberg, Musk and AI czar David Sacks.

▶

The order, as described by Axios, would have given federal agencies up to 90 days to review frontier model releases on security and safety grounds. The framework was reportedly voluntary, which is a meaningful caveat: this was much closer to a NIST style reporting regime than a binding pre approval. Even so, the lab CEOs who got the order pulled treated it as a forcing function that would have created a default expectation of compliance.

The political signal is bigger than the policy. The accelerationist coalition (Musk, Sacks, Zuckerberg, Andreessen) now appears to have effective veto power over US AI safety executive action through 2026, with the explicit framing that any regulatory friction risks ceding ground to China. That stance is contested inside the administration (some national security and intelligence officials reportedly back the order), but the public outcome is that the order is on ice.

Cross source check: Axios, NPR and CNN are the three primary sources, with each citing different administration officials. There is no direct quote from Trump beyond the "didn't like certain aspects" line. Treat the specifics of who lobbied whom as partially reported.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

Less regulatory friction at the federal level is mechanically a positive for AI infrastructure capex through 2026 because it reduces the probability that frontier model deployments get delayed for safety review. The risk to monitor is at the state level: California, Colorado and New York have all moved on AI procurement and disclosure rules in 2026, and a federal vacuum tends to accelerate state action. For CoreWeave that means watching California AB style frameworks rather than expecting a national overlay.
