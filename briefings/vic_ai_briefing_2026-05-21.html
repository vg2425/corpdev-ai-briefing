Google I/O 2026: Gemini 3.5 Flash, Omni video, Spark agent and Antigravity 2.0 mark a full pivot to agentic

Sundar Pichai opened I/O on May 19 framing the era as "agentic Gemini," with Gemini 3.5 Flash priced at $1.50 input / $9 output per million tokens, Gemini Omni as a unified video generation and editing model, and Gemini Spark as a 24/7 cloud resident personal agent rolling out to Ultra subscribers first.

▶

The model story has two parts. Gemini 3.5 Flash is the workhorse: Google claims it beats Gemini 3.1 Pro on coding and agentic benchmarks (76.2% Terminal-Bench 2.1, 83.6% MCP Atlas per llm-stats), priced roughly 40% cheaper than 3.1 Pro on both input and output. Pricing is 3x Gemini 3 Flash, which I would push back on as a real cost increase for high volume agentic apps even if VentureBeat's "more than $1B per year in enterprise savings" framing reads well. The savings claim assumes large workload migration from frontier tiers; the floor cost actually goes up if you were already running Flash.

Gemini Omni is the more interesting model release. It combines Gemini reasoning with Nano Banana, Veo and Genie into a single video generation and conversational editing system. Per Google's own blog, it can "anticipate what should happen next" in a clip and edit any element via language. If Omni is exposed as an API and not just inside the Gemini app and Flow, this is a direct competitive event for Runway, Pika and OpenAI's Sora line.

Spark is the agentic centerpiece. Google describes it as running 24/7 on Google Cloud VMs "even when your laptop is closed," with the ability to act across Workspace, third party apps and the web. Antigravity 2.0 is the developer surface that supports it, with new primitives for subagents, hooks and async task management. CNBC and Tom's Guide both note this is Google's most direct answer yet to OpenAI's Operator and to Claude as the default agent in code.

Cross source check: blog.google, CNBC, eWeek, Tom's Guide, MacRumors, VentureBeat, Cybernews, TechCrunch, HotHardware, Knightli.com and Constellation Research all align on the core lineup. The Gemini 3.5 Flash benchmarks are Google reported and have not been independently reproduced yet.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

Spark and Antigravity push Google further into the always on agentic envelope. Even if Google serves Gemini on its own TPUs, the surface area effect is what to track: every new agentic capability Google ships sets a new minimum viable product line for what enterprises expect agents to do across vendors. That pulls forward inference budgets at Anthropic, OpenAI and the open weights labs, all of which are the buyer set CoreWeave actually serves. Gemini 3.5 Flash pricing also gives Anthropic and OpenAI a more aggressive Flash tier comp to beat, which sharpens the per token margin question that drove the Krishna Rao podcast (see Podcast Picks).

Nvidia Q1 FY27: $81.6B revenue, data center alone hits $75.2B at +92% YoY, Q2 guide ~$91B

Nvidia reported on May 20 a record $81.6B in Q1 FY27 revenue (up 85% YoY), with the data center segment at $75.2B (up roughly 92%). Jensen Huang on the call: "Demand has gone parabolic. Agentic AI has arrived." Q2 guide of approximately $91B beat the ~$87B sell side consensus.

▶

This is the cleanest signal in the entire week that AI infrastructure spend has not peaked. A 92% YoY jump in data center revenue on a base that was already a record is a structurally different print from the late 2025 cycle. The free cash flow number reported by Electronics For You and Kiplinger ($49B at 74.9% gross margin) implies Nvidia is now self funding the buildout that everyone else is borrowing for.

One caveat worth flagging: the stock reaction was muted ahead of and immediately after the print, with Yahoo Finance reporting futures down on the back of the release. That tells me the buy side already priced this much in. The next test is whether Q2 actually beats the $91B guide, given the lead time on Blackwell and Vera Rubin allocations to large customers.

Huang's "Nvidia is the only platform that runs every frontier AI model" line, citing Anthropic, OpenAI, SpaceXAI, Meta and Google Gemini explicitly, is a deliberate response to the AMD MI400 noise from earlier in the quarter. The TPU and Trainium build outs at Google and Anthropic respectively are not flagged as competitive in any of the coverage I read, which is consistent with the "Nvidia plus a sliver" framing the market has settled on.

Cross source check: CNBC, Bloomberg, Fortune, Yahoo Finance, Kiplinger, Motley Fool, Electronics For You, moomoo Community and TradingKey all converge on the same headline numbers. The $49B FCF and 74.9% gross margin figures appear in multiple secondary sources but I have not located them in the Nvidia release text directly, so I would treat the exact gross margin number as approximate until verified against the 10-Q.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

Two cleanly opposing pressures. The positive read: $75.2B of data center revenue in one quarter means the supply of Blackwell and Vera Rubin into the market is enormous and accelerating, which is exactly the inflow CoreWeave's contracted backlog of $99.4B (Q1 print) is designed to monetize. The cautionary read: every neocloud, hyperscaler and mega tenant is now in the same allocation queue, and Nvidia's direct revenue gives it leverage to favor whichever customer concentration profile suits it. CoreWeave's positioning depends on staying in the top tier of preferred customers, not just on having the contracts. Worth watching whether the Q2 $91B guide includes named neocloud deliveries beyond IREN, OCI and CoreWeave itself.

OpenAI to confidentially file IPO as soon as this week, September debut targeted

CNBC, Axios, WSJ via Yahoo and others reported on May 20 that OpenAI plans to file a confidential draft S-1 with the SEC as soon as Friday May 22. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley are leading, the target is a September public debut, and OpenAI is currently valued by private investors at roughly $850B.

▶

The timing is not subtle. The filing comes two days after the Musk v Altman verdict (story 06) removed the last meaningful overhang on OpenAI's restructuring. CNBC's framing of OpenAI now leading the "AI IPO horse race" against SpaceX is the right read; the prediction markets visibly shifted within hours of the WSJ scoop.

One nuance the coverage glides past: confidential draft submissions typically precede a public S-1 by two to three months, and the actual offering by another month, which lines up with a September pricing window but leaves very little room for an October miss. If macro or compute cost surprises hit, this calendar is the constraint.

Cross source check: CNBC, Axios, Yahoo Finance, US News, Reuters via TradingView, ResultSense and EuropeSays all converge on the same Friday filing window and the same September target. The $850B private mark is from secondary trading data and is widely reported but I would treat it as a band rather than a precise number.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

An OpenAI IPO turns CoreWeave's largest single customer concentration into a public market disclosure event. The S-1 will have to itemize compute commitments and counterparty exposure, which will surface CoreWeave's role in OpenAI's training and inference stack to a broader investor audience than has seen it. That is asymmetric: every long oriented CoreWeave investor benefits from the visibility, but short oriented investors get a clean line to model OpenAI gross margin sensitivity to CoreWeave pricing. Worth pre reading the S-1 risk factors section closely the moment it goes public.

Karpathy defects to Anthropic to lead "Claude trains Claude" pre training research

Andrej Karpathy posted on May 19 that he has joined Anthropic, reporting to pre training lead Nick Joseph. His mandate: use Claude to accelerate pre training research itself, the first serious internal lab bet on operational recursive self improvement.

▶

The signal value here exceeds the personnel value, and the personnel value is already very high. Karpathy is the most legible AI engineer in the world on X, and his move from OpenAI co founder to Tesla Autopilot to independent education to Anthropic frames the relative pull of the labs in 2026. Algorithmic Bridge, Fortune and Inc all read this the same way: this is the most public defection in the cycle.

The technical framing matters more than the headline. "Using Claude to accelerate pre training research" is concrete RSI work: Claude generates and evaluates training experiments, data curation pipelines and architecture tweaks, with humans in a steering role rather than a doing role. TechCrunch explicitly notes Anthropic's framing of "preparing Claude to improve itself." That is the same thesis the just-funded Recursive Superintelligence (story 07) is built around, and it puts Anthropic publicly on that path.

I want to flag two things to push back on in the coverage. First, "recursive self improvement" is being used loosely across outlets; what Karpathy is actually doing is more accurately "model assisted research acceleration," which is closer to GPT-style internal evals than to any hard takeoff scenario. Second, several outlets are treating this as Anthropic having "out hired" OpenAI; I would treat that as one data point in a much noisier hiring market.

Cross source check: TechCrunch, VentureBeat, Fortune, Inc, Yahoo, Algorithmic Bridge and Let's Data Science all confirm the same role and reporting line. Karpathy's X post is the primary source.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

Pre training research that uses Claude to accelerate Claude implies materially more compute consumed per unit of model progress, not less. Even an efficient model assisted research loop runs more experiments more often, and those experiments need GPU capacity outside the customer serving fleet. Anthropic's $100B AWS commitment and growing CoreWeave footprint both look more durable if internal research compute scales alongside customer inference. Track Anthropic capex commentary in subsequent earnings adjacent disclosures (the company is private, but Krishna Rao on ILTB this week confirmed roughly $75B raised cumulatively).

Motley Fool and TradingKey are both more cautious than TechCrunch on the $66B mark relative to actual revenue run rate; that is a more useful read than the day one tape.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

Cerebras is not a direct competitor to CoreWeave, but its IPO sets the comp for how the public market values AI infrastructure with concentrated customer exposure. The $66B mark on what is still substantially a single tenant or low diversification revenue book recalibrates how analysts will value CoreWeave's $99.4B backlog. The deeper point: every successful AI infra IPO this year widens the universe of public capital willing to underwrite the AI factory thesis, which lowers cost of capital across the category. Watch how CoreWeave's convertible or follow on issuance terms move over the next 60 days against the new Cerebras comp set.

🔥 High Signal Policy Business 5 sources

Jury delivers unanimous verdict for OpenAI in Musk case; statute of limitations bars all claims

On May 19 a jury in Oakland deliberated less than two hours and unanimously found that Musk's claims against OpenAI, Sam Altman and Greg Brockman were time barred. Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers dismissed the case. Musk's counsel Marc Toberoff: "Appeal."

▶

The legal narrowing is the cleanest part. The jury did not rule on the substantive question of whether OpenAI broke its founding nonprofit covenants, only that Musk waited too long to bring suit. That preserves the possibility of future cases on similar grounds from other plaintiffs, but it removes the most consequential active legal threat to OpenAI's for profit restructuring.

Axios's framing that "the entire AI industry lost" is rhetorical, and I would push back on it: the industry actually gained predictability. OpenAI's IPO calendar (story 03) was the immediate beneficiary, and the speed with which OpenAI moved on the IPO filing two days later suggests this was the gating event for the bankers.

Cross source check: CNN, Al Jazeera, Axios, Newsweek, CNBC and Reuters via secondary sources all converge on the jury composition, deliberation length, and the statute of limitations finding. Musk's appeal threat is single sourced (Toberoff comment outside the courthouse) and is reported uniformly.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

The OpenAI overhang is the single biggest customer level risk that sits above CoreWeave's revenue concentration story. With the suit dismissed and an IPO filing imminent, OpenAI is on a much cleaner financial trajectory, which de risks the duration of CoreWeave's largest contracted revenue stream. The second order effect: with this litigation cleared, OpenAI is freer to sign longer term, larger capacity commitments with infrastructure providers, since boards no longer need to underwrite that legal liability into the counterparty contract.

Recursive Superintelligence exits stealth with $650M at $4.65B to chase self improving AI

London based Recursive came out of stealth on May 13 with $650M led by GV and Greycroft, with Nvidia and AMD also participating, at a $4.65B valuation. The team is under 30 people, drawn from OpenAI, DeepMind, Meta, Salesforce and Uber AI. Public model launch targeted for mid 2026.

▶

This is the second public bet of the week on the RSI thesis, alongside the Karpathy hire at Anthropic (story 04). The fact that both Nvidia and AMD are in the round is unusual and worth flagging: GPU vendors typically pick a side. Both signing the cap table suggests a research compute lock in pitch ("we will use whatever chips work best") rather than an exclusive supply story.

The narrative claim to push back on: "self improving AI" is doing a lot of work. The TechCrunch and Unite.AI write ups soften it correctly to "AI that analyses its own performance and proposes improvements," with humans still in the loop. There is no credible technical path to fully autonomous recursive improvement in mid 2026 timelines, and treating this as one is the kind of framing the round depends on but the engineering will not deliver in twelve months.

Cross source check: Tech.eu, The Decoder, TechCrunch, Unite.AI, Crowdfund Insider and TFN all converge on the $650M, $4.65B valuation, and investor list. The "less than 30 staff" figure is consistent across the European outlets.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

A $650M raise into a sub 30 person research lab is almost entirely a compute pre purchase. That capital has very few homes other than reserved GPU capacity, and the participation of Nvidia and AMD makes the supply side of that conversation easier. Sub frontier labs like this are exactly the customer tier where CoreWeave's flexibility, reserved capacity model and managed services pitch lands hardest. Worth a direct outreach if not already in motion; their compute footprint will materialize fast.

Pope Leo XIV's first encyclical Magnifica humanitas drops May 25, presented alongside an Anthropic co founder

The Vatican confirmed on May 14 that Pope Leo XIV will publish his first encyclical, titled Magnifica humanitas, on May 25. The document focuses on safeguarding the human person in the age of artificial intelligence. Chris Olah of Anthropic will speak on the launch panel.

▶

The framing is deliberate. Leo XIV signed the document on May 15, the 135th anniversary of Leo XIII's Rerum novarum, the encyclical that defined Catholic social teaching during industrialization. The intentional parallel is that AI is being positioned by the Vatican as the equivalent civilizational reordering of this century. The panel inclusion of Anthropic's Chris Olah (an interpretability researcher rather than a policy executive) is unusual and signals a genuine effort to engage technical builders, not just to issue a critique.

I have not read the encyclical text yet (it publishes on the 25th), so all current coverage is based on prior Leo statements and pre briefings. Treat any specific policy recommendations sourced to it before May 25 as unverified.

Cross source check: Vatican News, Axios, National Catholic Reporter, America Magazine and Angelus News all confirm the title, publication date, and the Olah appearance. The signature date of May 15 is consistent across sources.

Why It Matters for CoreWeave

This is the second order policy story of the year. Religious institutions are not regulators, but encyclicals shape the elite consensus that European and Latin American regulators draw from when drafting AI rules, and they shape board level conversations at customers in regulated sectors (healthcare, education, defense). For an infrastructure provider, the read is indirect but real: workloads in sensitive verticals will increasingly need to demonstrate audit trails, data governance and human oversight features. CoreWeave's positioning around higher tier managed services and security guarantees becomes more commercially valuable as that narrative spreads.
