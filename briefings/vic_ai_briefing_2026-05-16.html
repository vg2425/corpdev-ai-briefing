Cerebras IPO opens 2026 with a bang: shares pop 68% on Nasdaq debut, market cap hits $95B

Wafer scale AI chipmaker Cerebras (CBRS) priced 30M shares at $185, raised $5.55B, and closed up roughly 68% on May 14, the largest US tech IPO since Uber in 2019. Demand was reported as more than 20x oversubscribed.

Cerebras priced well above its raised range of $150 to $160, valuing the company near $40B before the open. By the close on May 14 the stock had hit roughly $311, putting market cap near $95B. Shares pulled back on May 15 in normal post IPO churn.

The print matters for two reasons. First, it reopens the AI hardware IPO window after a long quiet stretch and resets the comp set for any AI compute issuer planning a 2026 listing. Second, it puts a public market price on non Nvidia training and inference silicon at scale, which has been mostly private until now (Groq, Tenstorrent, Etched).

Open question: how much of the day one move was scarcity (only 30M shares free float) versus a real re rating of inference silicon. Watch the next two earnings prints and the lockup expiry for the cleaner read.

Why it matters for CoreWeave

Cerebras is positioned for ultra fast inference on its CS systems, not GPU substitution, so this is less a direct competitive event and more a valuation tailwind. A blowout AI infra IPO validates the multiples public markets are willing to pay for AI capacity exposure, which supports CoreWeave's own narrative. Worth tracking: will Cerebras try to compete for the same large inference contracts CoreWeave is winning on Blackwell and Rubin, or stay in its specialty lane.

Google and SpaceX in talks to launch orbital AI data centers (Project Suncatcher)

WSJ first reported, and Bloomberg and TechCrunch confirmed, that Google is in advanced discussions with SpaceX about lifting TPU clusters into orbit. Google's "Project Suncatcher" prototype satellites are targeted for 2027.

The concept parks TPUs on solar powered satellites, using near constant sunlight for power and the cold of space as a passive heat sink. SpaceX is reportedly pitching this as the cheapest place to put AI compute "within a few years," which is part of the narrative supporting its planned $1.75T IPO later in 2026.

Skeptics are vocal. Amazon's cloud chief publicly said orbital data centers are "nowhere close" to practical once you load in launch cost, radiation hardening, and on orbit servicing. TechCrunch's analysis lines up with that view for now.

Even discounted, this is a real signal of how hyperscalers are starting to think about power constraints. Earth side power is the hard ceiling on AI buildout, and any credible alternative shifts the long term competitive map.

Why it matters for CoreWeave

Orbital compute is not a near term threat. Terrestrial GPU clouds remain dramatically cheaper per FLOP, and CoreWeave's 3.5 GW of contracted power and Blackwell/Rubin Roadmap are the right bet for the next 3 to 5 years. But it tells you what the world's biggest buyer of compute is willing to entertain when grid interconnect timelines are 4+ years. That makes CoreWeave's executed power contracts and shovel ready sites a more strategic asset than they look on paper.

Anthropic's Mythos fallout continues: Palo Alto warns of a 3 to 5 month window for AI driven exploits

Mid May coverage focused on the security aftermath of Anthropic's Mythos preview. Palo Alto Networks publicly warned enterprises have a "narrow three to five month window" to harden defenses before AI driven attacks become the new norm.

Mythos was previewed in April and is described by Anthropic as the first model that could plausibly take down a Fortune 100 from a workstation, with strong zero day discovery across major OSes and browsers. Anthropic limited access to roughly 40 vetted security partners under Project Glasswing.

This week's story is the reaction. CNBC reported that bank security teams are restructuring around the Mythos threat model. Palo Alto Networks set a hard timeline for defensive readiness. Reports also surfaced that hackers obtained Mythos access through a third party vendor, though Anthropic says its own systems were not breached.

On May 11, CNBC reported that OpenAI will give EU regulators early access to a comparable cyber capable model while Anthropic has not yet matched that commitment. That is becoming a policy divergence worth watching.

Why it matters for CoreWeave

Two implications. First, defensive AI workloads are about to be a real inference category. Security teams running 24/7 AI driven detection and patch generation against frontier models will need premium, low latency GPU capacity, which is CoreWeave's sweet spot. Second, model deployment will get more regulated. Customers running cyber capable models will likely demand evidence of provider side controls (isolation, audit, evidence of trust), which favors specialized providers over commodity GPU rental.

Google Cloud Next 2026: TPU 8t/8i, Virgo Network fabric, and Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform

Google unveiled its 8th generation TPUs in two variants (8t for training, 8i for inference), a new megascale data center fabric called Virgo Network, and a Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform. Google said over half of 2026 ML compute investment is going to the Cloud business.

TPU 8t scales up to 9,600 chips and 2 PB of shared HBM in a single superpod. TPU 8i pods connect 1,152 chips with 3x more on chip SRAM, sold as the inference workhorse for "millions of concurrent agents." Virgo is the underlying fabric.

The strategic frame is that Google is now selling a full stack agent runtime: TPU 8i for inference economics, Gemini 3 Flash as the default model, and an enterprise agent platform sitting on top. The pitch is to undercut a "stack three things together" approach by selling one end to end product.

Important nuance: Google's TurboQuant KV cache compression (announced March) materially improves the economics of long context inference on TPUs. That is the algorithmic complement to TPU 8i.

Why it matters for CoreWeave

This is the clearest "custom silicon eats Nvidia" announcement of the year. The risk for any Nvidia GPU pure play is that a subset of agent inference workloads, especially long context with low margin economics, migrates to TPU 8i. Counterweight: TPUs lock customers into Google Cloud, so non Google customers (most of the Fortune 500, every sovereign cloud buyer, every regulated industry) still need Nvidia capacity outside hyperscalers. That is precisely CoreWeave's positioning.
